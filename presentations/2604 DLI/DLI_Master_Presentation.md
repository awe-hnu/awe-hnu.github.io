## There Is No Recipe

Cyber attacks. AI transformation. Platform shifts.

There is no recipe.

**What you need: a toolbox.**

<!-- IMAGE: toolbox_three_compartments.jpg - A clean, open toolbox with three clearly distinct compartments or drawers, photographed top-down or at slight angle, representing mental models / methods / social skills -->

<!-- SPEAKER NOTES:
Let me open with a question. Who learned in their bachelor's program how to design a cyber resilience strategy for a company that doesn't exist yet? [Pause.] Or how to lead an organization through an AI transformation when the technology itself is still changing week by week?

No one. Because there's no recipe for that yet.

And this is the central challenge of IT leadership today: you will walk into situations where the standard playbook doesn't apply. The problem is genuinely new, the stakes are high, and someone has to figure it out.

That someone needs a toolbox, not a recipe book. A toolbox with three compartments.

First: mental models (theories and frameworks that help you penetrate complexity; not memorized answers, but lenses that let you see problems differently). Second: methods (proven practices for moving from a fuzzy problem to a defensible solution). Third: social skills (the ability to lead, persuade, and collaborate, because none of this happens alone).

Building that toolbox is what the DLI is about. You'll see this image come back throughout the presentation; it's the central idea.
-->

## Four Principles, Three Compartments

Think in Systems · Decide with Evidence → **Mental Models**

Learn by Doing Research → **Methods**

Lead from Day One → **Social Skills**

<!-- IMAGE: four_pillars_toolbox.jpg - A toolbox open with three labeled compartments (Mental Models / Methods / Social Skills), with small icons or objects in each representing the DLI principles -->

<!-- SPEAKER NOTES:
Let me make the connection explicit, because this is the intellectual core of the program.

The four principles you'll hear about DLI (Think in Systems, Decide with Evidence, Learn by Doing Research, Lead from Day One) are not separate marketing claims. They describe how we fill the three compartments of the toolbox.

"Think in Systems" means that when you're working on a cyber resilience challenge, you don't just look at the firewall or the software stack. You map the suppliers, the employees, the regulatory environment, the dependencies you haven't thought of yet. That's a mental model at work.

"Decide with Evidence" means your recommendations are grounded in data you gathered and analyzed yourself, not gut feeling, not "best practice" copied from a consultant deck.

"Learn by Doing Research" is about the methods compartment. You don't take a standalone "research methods" class and then forget it. You encounter research methods in the act of solving real problems. The method becomes meaningful when it answers a question that matters.

"Lead from Day One" is the social skills compartment. From the first semester, you present, you defend, you collaborate under pressure. You don't get a "leadership simulation" in semester four; you're already doing it.

## What's in Your Toolbox at the End

Decompose complex IT problems

Build evidence-based solutions under uncertainty

Defend solutions to leadership and team

Independently navigate the state of the art

<!-- IMAGE: filled_toolbox.jpg - The same toolbox from slide 2, now filled: each compartment contains visible, recognizable tools: a magnifying glass / compass for mental models, structured documents / frameworks for methods, and figures in conversation for social skills -->

<!-- SPEAKER NOTES:
Let me translate this into concrete capabilities (not learning objectives from a module handbook, but what you can actually do when you walk out of here).

One: you can take a complex, messy IT problem (the kind where the first challenge is just understanding what the problem actually is) and decompose it into tractable pieces. That's the mental models compartment working.

Two: you can design and execute a path from that decomposed problem to a solution that stands up to scrutiny. Not an opinion; a solution with evidence behind it. That's methods.

Three: you can walk into a room of senior executives and make the case for the strategy you developed: defend it under questioning, adjust to objections, persuade people who weren't in the room when you gathered the evidence. That's social skills.

Four: you can pick up a new topic (a technology area, a management challenge, a regulatory development) and independently map what is known, what is contested, and where the frontier is. That's the research capability underneath all three compartments.

These four capabilities travel with you regardless of what industry you end up in or what role you grow into.

## Two Environments, One Goal

Lectures and seminars: structured mental models

Research projects: real problems, real partners

Both, not either

<!-- IMAGE: hnu_lecture_and_lab.jpg - Split image or diptych: left side a lecture room or seminar setting, right side a working session or whiteboard workshop with a company partner -->

<!-- SPEAKER NOTES:
Here's where the toolbox idea becomes structural.

Lectures alone stay abstract. You learn a framework, you pass an exam, you forget it. Practice alone stays shallow; you solve a problem, but you don't know why your solution worked, and you can't transfer the learning to the next problem.

The DLI weaves both. The lectures and seminars give you the structured mental models (the lenses). The research projects apply those lenses to real challenges with real industry partners from semester one.

[One sentence for insiders:] Our lab-based accreditation rather than module-based accreditation is what makes this flexibility possible; it lets us keep content current as the field moves. But that's a technical detail. What matters to you is: you won't wait until semester three to touch a real problem.

Topics shift. The toolbox principle stays.

<!-- IMAGE: cyber_and_agentic.jpg - Two halves: left a stylized network/shield motif representing cyber resilience; right a network of connected AI agents or nodes representing agentic AI; clean and forward-looking -->

<!-- SPEAKER NOTES:
For the Winter 2026 cohort (which is you), the two thematic anchors are Cyber Resilience and Agentic AI.

Cyber Resilience: this is not just about preventing breaches. It's about designing organizations and systems that remain operational when something gets through; eventually, something will. This field is evolving fast and involves IT architecture, organizational design, regulatory compliance, and crisis leadership simultaneously. No single discipline owns it yet.

Agentic AI: autonomous systems are reshaping how work gets done at every level. The strategic, organizational, and leadership questions around agentic AI are genuinely open. We don't have settled frameworks. We're building them.

Both fields share a characteristic that makes them ideal for the DLI approach: recipe knowledge doesn't yet exist. The people who will shape these fields over the next decade are exactly the people who develop the toolbox now.
