What constitutes digital innovation?

Digital Innovation

Hund et al. (2021) offers following definitions:

  • Digital objects are enduring, structured collections of elements that include one or more bitstrings, which follow a set of syntactical rules.
  • Digital technology is a digital object that has been assigned a socially agreed-upon meaning. A digital object becomes a digital technology when it is assigned a meaning, namely a purpose for applying it, whereby the purpose is determined by social actors such as users.
  • Digital innovation refers to the creation or adoption, and exploitation of an inherently unbounded, value-adding novelty (e.g., product, service, process, or business model) through the incorporation of digital technology

Digitization, digitalization and value creation

Yoo, Henfridsson, and Lyytinen (2010) identify three characteristics of digital innovation as key: homogenization of data, reprogrammability, and self-referentiality.

  • Homogenization of data by means of digitization makes any form of digital content accessible to any kind of digital device, thereby, separating content and medium.
  • Reprogrammability allows a digital device to perform an almost unlimited number of functions as long as users have to agree upon a shared meaning of the data.
  • Self-reference is expressed by the use of digital technology to create positive network externalities “that further accelerate the creation and availability of digital devices, networks, services, and contents” which “in turn, fosters further digital innovation through a virtuous cycle of lowered entry barriers, decreased learning costs, and accelerated diffusion rates” (Yoo, Henfridsson, and Lyytinen 2010, 726).

Objects, digital objects, digital technology and digital innovation are related by following mechanisms:

  • Digitization is “the process of converting analog signals into a digital form, and ultimately into binary digits (bits)” (Tilson, Lyytinen, and Sørensen 2010, 749).
  • Digitalization refers to “[…] a sociotechnical process of applying digitizing techniques to broader social and institutional contexts that render digital technologies infrastructural” (Tilson, Lyytinen, and Sørensen 2010, 749).
  • Novel value creation refers to a process that adds value to the sociotechnical environment (e.g., individual behavior, market offerings, business processes) in a novel way (Hund et al. 2021).
  • Ontological reversal is where the digital version is created first, and the physical version second (if needed)” (Baskerville, Myers, and Yoo 2020, 509).

How do digital infrastructures, digital platforms, digital ecosystems differ?

Infrastructure, platform, and ecosystem

Hund et al. (2021) offers following definitions:

Digital infrastructures are the technical foundation upon which digital platforms (i.e., “the basic information technologies and organizational structures, along with the related services and facilities necessary for an enterprise or industry to function” (Tilson, Lyytinen, and Sørensen 2010, 748)).

Digital platforms allow various actors to develop modules such as add-on software as they are the “extensible codebase of a software-based system that provides core functionality shared by the modules that interoperate with it and the interfaces through which they interoperate” (Tiwana, Konsynski, and Bush 2010, 676).

Digital ecosystems refer to the “collection of the platform and the modules specific to it” (Tiwana, Konsynski, and Bush 2010, 676).

What other paradoxes do you see?

Paradoxes

Hund et al. (2021) highlights three paradoxes related to digital technology:

  • The paradox of openness relates to the tension between value creation and value appropriation. Organizations may open their platforms to engage with a broad set of external actors and enable their innovation activities (i.e., value creation), but likewise have to protect their own firm’s knowledge from being copied by competitors (i.e., value appropriation).
  • The paradox of change captures the persistent contradiction between stability (to allow enrolling new actors and developments) versus flexibility (to allow unbounded growth) .
  • The paradox of control captures the contradiction between enabling individual autonomy through decentralized control to foster generativity, while maintaining centralized control over the platform or infrastructure.
