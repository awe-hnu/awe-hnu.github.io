135 minutes total.

There is no specific pre-reading for this unit. The activation is experience-based: students reflect on their best and worst team experiences.

  • The activation uses a World Café format — three rounds at rotating tables, each with a different prompt about team experience.
  • The team development section uses an expert panel simulation — each group becomes an expert on one stage and presents to the class.
  • Psychological safety uses a muddiest point collection — students anonymously submit their most confusing point, then work collaboratively to resolve them.
  • The case exercise uses a fishbowl role-play — an inner group acts out the scenario while the outer ring observes and diagnoses.

Learning objectives

After completing this unit, you will be able to:

  1. Explain team development stages and the shifting leadership role across them
  2. Define psychological safety and describe its impact on team learning and performance
  3. Analyse the diversity paradox and the role of inclusion in activating diverse teams
  4. Propose leadership practices for building trust and effectiveness in virtual and distributed teams

Team development

Team effectiveness

According to Hill (2003), an effective team is characterized by three criteria:

  1. The team performs: the output meets the standards of those who have to use it
  2. The team members are satisfied and learn: the experience contributes to each member’s well-being and development
  3. The team adapts and learns: the experience enhances the capability of members to work together in the future

Short-term results alone do not define a successful team — sustainability and growth matter equally.

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Emphasize that most organizations focus only on criterion 1 (performance). But a team that delivers results while burning out its members or failing to build capacity for the future is not truly effective.

Connect to engaging leadership (Unit 4): engaging leaders attend to all three criteria — facilitating performance, strengthening competence, and connecting members.

Team paradoxes

Just as organizations face paradoxes (Unit 3), so do teams (Hill, 2003):

  • Embrace individual differences ⭤ Embrace collective identity and goals
  • Foster support ⭤ Foster confrontation
  • Focus on performance ⭤ Focus on learning and development
  • Rely on managerial authority ⭤ Rely on team members’ discretion and autonomy

Navigating these paradoxes requires behavioral complexity — moving across the CVF quadrants depending on what the team needs.

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Connect to Unit 3: these are the same paradox dynamics, now applied at the team level. The CVF quadrants map directly: collaborate ↔︎ compete, create ↔︎ control. No single quadrant suffices for complex team leadership.

Tuckman’s stages

Tuckman (1965) proposed that teams progress through predictable stages:

Forming —Storming —Norming —Performing— Adjourning

Mini-lecture (5 min).

Walk through each stage briefly:

  • Forming: politeness, uncertainty, dependence on the leader.
  • Storming: conflict emerges, members assert perspectives. Most uncomfortable but most critical.
  • Norming: shared norms, roles, and ways of working develop.
  • Performing: high-level functioning, interdependence, flexibility.
  • Adjourning: reflection, celebration, sometimes grief.

Limitation: implies linear progression that many teams don’t follow. Teams can regress when facing new challenges.

Wheelan’s Integrated Model

The IMGD (Wheelan, 2005) provides an empirically grounded refinement:

Stage Focus Key behaviors
Stage 1: Dependency & inclusion “Will I belong?” Members look to leader for direction
Stage 2: Counterdependency & fight “Who has influence?” Members challenge leader and each other
Stage 3: Trust & structure “How do we work together?” Mature negotiation of roles and norms
Stage 4: Work & productivity “Let’s get it done.” Effective collaboration, flexible roles

Mini-lecture (5 min).

Key nuances beyond Tuckman:

  1. Teams don’t progress linearly — they can regress when facing membership changes or new tasks.
  2. Most teams never reach Stage 4.
  3. Time matters — teams need sufficient time together.
  4. The leader’s role shifts across stages.

This shifting role connects to situational leadership (Unit 3): directive → coaching → supporting → delegating maps onto development stages.

The leader’s role across stages

Team stage Leadership approach Key actions
Dependency & inclusion Directive Provide clear structure, establish safety
Counterdependency & fight Coaching Normalize conflict, facilitate dialogue
Trust & structure Supporting Encourage self-management, delegate decisions
Work & productivity Delegating Manage boundaries, secure resources

Mini-lecture (3 min).

This maps onto the situational leadership model from Unit 3 (Hersey & Blanchard, 1977). The leader does not become less important — but the nature of the contribution changes from providing answers to creating conditions.

Also adaptive leadership in action: in early stages, challenges are mostly technical. As the team matures, challenges become more adaptive.

Stage diagnosis exercise

What stage is this team in?

Read each brief scenario and diagnose: What Wheelan stage? What leadership approach is needed?

  1. Team members defer all decisions to the project manager. Nobody pushes back.
  2. Two subgroups have formed and argue about the project approach in every meeting.
  3. The team has developed clear sprint rituals and members freely pick up each other’s tasks.
12:00

Expert panel simulation (12 min total: 6 min prep, 6 min performance).

Assign each group ONE Wheelan stage (Stages 1–4). Each group becomes the “expert panel” on their stage and must: (1) diagnose which of the three scenarios matches their stage, (2) explain what the leader should do, (3) warn what happens if the wrong approach is used.

Give 6 minutes for preparation. Then each panel presents in 90 seconds — standing at the front, fielding one question from the audience. The expert panel format creates a sense of authority and accountability that standard group work lacks.

Expected answers: 1. Stage 1 (Dependency) — needs directive leadership, clear structure 2. Stage 2 (Counterdependency) — needs coaching, normalize the conflict 3. Stage 4 (Work & productivity) — needs delegating, boundary management

Transition: “What enables teams to move from Stage 2 to Stage 3? The answer, research shows, is psychological safety.”

Psychological safety

Definition

Psychological safety is a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking.

Edmondson (1999) showed that psychological safety enables learning behavior — asking questions, admitting mistakes, seeking feedback, experimenting — which drives team performance.

Mini-lecture (5 min).

The research began with a puzzling finding: the best-performing hospital teams reported more errors. It turned out they had higher psychological safety, so members felt safe to report and discuss errors — leading to more learning and fewer repeated mistakes.

Key insight: psychological safety is not about being nice. It is about creating an environment where people feel safe to be candid.

Project Aristotle

Google’s Project Aristotle confirmed and extended these findings:

Psychological safety was the #1 predictor of team effectiveness at Google.

The five key dynamics (in order):

  1. Psychological safety — Can we take risks without feeling insecure?
  2. Dependability — Can we count on each other?
  3. Structure & clarity — Are goals, roles, and plans clear?
  4. Meaning — Is the work personally important?
  5. Impact — Do we believe our work matters?

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Project Aristotle studied 180+ teams over two years. They expected team composition to be key — instead, how teams worked together mattered far more than who was on the team.

This reinforces a key course message: leadership is not just about selecting the right people (traits, Unit 2) — it is about creating the right conditions.

How leaders create psychological safety

  1. Model vulnerability — Acknowledge your own mistakes. When the leader says “I don’t know,” it signals fallibility is acceptable.
  2. Frame failure as learning — Ask “What can we learn?” rather than “Who is responsible?”
  3. Invite input actively — Don’t just have an “open door” — actively solicit perspectives, especially from quieter members.
  4. Respond productively — How the leader responds to the first person who speaks up determines whether others follow.

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Creating psychological safety is a leadership practice, not a one-time event. A single punitive response to a mistake can destroy months of trust-building.

Connect to engaging leadership (Unit 4): facilitating (autonomy), connecting (relatedness), and strengthening (competence) all contribute to psychological safety. Disengaging leadership destroys it.

Psychological safety ≠ comfort zone

Low accountability High accountability
High psychological safety Comfort zone Learning zone
Low psychological safety Apathy zone Anxiety zone

The goal is the learning zone: high psychological safety combined with high standards.

05:00

How would you move a team from the anxiety zone to the learning zone?

Muddiest point + collaborative resolution (7 min).

Phase 1 — Muddiest point (2 min): Hand out index cards. Each student writes their most confusing or provocative question about psychological safety so far. Collect the cards and read 3–4 aloud (anonymity lowers the barrier to admitting confusion — itself a psychological safety exercise).

Phase 2 — Collaborative resolution (5 min): For each muddiest point, ask: “Who can help resolve this?” Let students answer each other’s questions. Only intervene if the class gets stuck. This peer-teaching moment embodies the learning zone: high accountability (you must explain) + high safety (questions are anonymous and welcomed).

If no “muddy” points emerge, fall back to: “What does it look like in practice to have high standards AND high safety?”

The distinction is critical: psychological safety is sometimes misunderstood as “being nice.” In fact, the most effective teams combine safety with rigorous standards. This connects to team paradoxes: foster support ⭤ foster confrontation.

Diversity & inclusion

The diversity paradox

Diverse teams have higher potential and higher process loss.

The diversity paradox: diversity increases perspectives and ideas, but also increases potential for misunderstanding and conflict.

  • Diverse teams are better at creative solutions and avoiding groupthink
  • But they can experience more communication breakdowns and slower decisions
  • The difference between high-performing and low-performing diverse teams is inclusion

Mini-lecture (5 min).

Simply assembling a diverse team is not enough — and can backfire without inclusive practices. Without psychological safety, diverse members may self-censor or disengage. The team bears the coordination costs without reaping cognitive benefits.

This is why psychological safety is a prerequisite for diversity to work.

Inclusion as leadership practice

Inclusion is what activates the potential of diversity:

  • Voice equity — ensuring all members have equal opportunity to contribute
  • Perspective-taking — actively seeking to understand others’ viewpoints
  • Belonging cues — small but powerful signals that each member is valued
  • Constructive dissent norms — making it normal to disagree respectfully

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Inclusion connects directly to psychological safety — inclusion is psychological safety applied to the challenge of diversity. A psychologically safe but homogeneous team misses innovation benefits. A diverse but unsafe team suppresses the very perspectives that make diversity valuable.

Inclusion scenario exercise

Analyse and propose interventions.

Scenario A: In a product team, the three engineers dominate technical discussions while two designers rarely speak. Recent design decisions have been technically sound but user experience has suffered.

Scenario B: A global marketing team has members from 5 countries. Video calls are always led by the US-based members, with Asian team members rarely contributing until asked directly.

For each: What is the inclusion problem? What specific practices would you implement?

08:00

Group exercise (12 min: 8 min groups, 4 min plenary).

Expected analysis: - Scenario A: Voice equity problem. Solutions: rotate facilitation, structured input rounds, separate design reviews. - Scenario B: Cultural + power distance issue. Solutions: async input before calls, rotating time zones, explicit norms for contribution, check-ins with quieter members.

Push groups to connect solutions to specific concepts: psychological safety, engaging leadership, CVF quadrants.

Virtual teams & case exercise

Swift trust

Meyerson et al. (1996) introduced swift trust for virtual teams:

In the absence of time for traditional trust-building, members make an initial decision to act as if trust exists.

Swift trust is fragile: it must be confirmed through early interactions and consistent follow-through. A single violation can shatter it.

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Swift trust explains why some virtual teams perform well from the start while others struggle. Teams that begin with clear role expectations, professional introductions, and early quick wins maintain trust. Teams that begin with ambiguity and missed deadlines quickly lose it.

For leaders: the first interactions in a virtual team are disproportionately important.

Practical virtual team practices

  • Structured communication rhythms — regular check-ins, stand-ups, retrospectives
  • Explicit norms — make implicit expectations explicit (response times, camera policies, decision-making)
  • Intentional relationship building — deliberate space for non-task interaction
  • Technology choices with purpose — match tool to message type

Mini-lecture (3 min).

Leading virtual teams requires more deliberate, structured leadership than co-located teams. What happens naturally in a shared office must be intentionally designed.

Connect to the latticework: understanding team stages helps diagnose virtual team struggles. Understanding psychological safety helps design meeting practices. Understanding SDT (Unit 4) helps create autonomy without losing connection.

Integrated case exercise

Diagnose the team and propose interventions.

A newly formed cross-functional project team has been tasked with developing a digital customer portal. Members from IT, marketing, customer service, and finance — some co-located, some remote. After three weeks:

  • The IT and marketing members frequently clash over priorities
  • Remote members rarely speak up in meetings
  • The team has missed its first milestone
  • One senior member dominates discussions
15:00

Fishbowl role-play (15 min: 5 min casting + prep, 5 min performance, 5 min outer-ring debrief).

Select 5 volunteers for the inner circle. Assign roles: project leader, IT member, marketing member, remote team member, dominant senior member. Give them 3 minutes to prepare their character’s perspective. The project leader must run a 5-minute team meeting — the others stay in character.

The outer ring observes silently with a diagnostic checklist: - What Wheelan stage is this team in? - Where is psychological safety breaking down? - What inclusion problems are visible? - What should the leader do differently?

After the role-play, the outer ring provides diagnosis and advice (5 min). Compare diagnoses: “Did you all see the same stage? The same problems?” Differences in diagnosis illustrate that team leadership requires multiple lenses.

Expected diagnosis: Stage 2 (counterdependency & fight), low psychological safety, unmanaged diversity, virtual team inclusion problems.
